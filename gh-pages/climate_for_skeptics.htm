Climate Change for USA Skeptics

 

 

We are more likely than not in the midst of the sixth great extinction event in the history of life on earth. Droughts, floods, plagues, disease, extinctions, food and water shortages, human migrations – it could truly be biblical in proportion. However, if the global community acts now, it has the capability to significantly reduce the impact of climate change.

 

Climate Change

The earth is getting warmer, the sea level is rising, and Ocean pH is decreasing (becoming more acidic). Total Solar Irradiation (TSI) has decreased over the last 50 years, yet the earth continues to warm. Looking at the long-term climate patterns (Paleoclimatic), the earth will be in a cooling phase for the next 9,500 years. The earth is defying the short and long-term climate cycles and getting warmer because of increased levels of green house gases, primarily carbon dioxide (CO2), in the atmosphere.

Skeptics argue that changes in temperature and CO2 levels are a normal part of the global climate cycle. What’s overlooked is where we are on the climate cycles. Long term, we expect warming effects if the Earth’s Obliquity and Eccentricity are at their maximums, and the Earth’s Precession has the Northern Hemisphere summer when Earth is closest to the sun (Perihelion) [there’s more land mass in the North to absorb heat]. The Current Obliquity is 23.4 degrees and decreasing, and is expected to hit its minimum in year 10,000. The current eccentricity is .0167 and decreasing, and will hit its minimum in year 28,000. As for Precession, the current Northern Hemisphere summer occurs when earth is furthest from the Sun (aphelion). The Northern Hemisphere summer will next occur when the earth is closest to the sun near the year 11,500.  [See the appendix for the definition of orbital terms and for a reference to calculate the orbital parameters over time.]

 

In the short-term, the global temperature lags solar irradiation levels by a month or two. Over the last 50 years, solar irradiation has been declining yet the global mean temperature has been increasing, see chart below. The only rational explanation for the temperature increase with lower irradiation (and the current orbital parameters) is the greenhouse effect, given the high levels of CO2 in the atmosphere. Carbon dioxide acts as a blanket and radiates infrared heat that would normally escape into space, back to earth. The greenhouse effect is essential to life on earth, however the blanket is getting too thick and too much heat is being radiated back to earth, increasing the global mean temperature [see http://climate.nasa.gov/causes/ for a more detailed explanation].

 

 

 

 

The ocean absorbs 30% of CO2 from the atmosphere, creating carbonic acid and therefore lowering the pH of seawater.  The chart below shows recent trends of Ocean CO2 and pH level, note that pH uses a negative log10 scale. By year 2100 the ocean pH is expected to drop another .06 to .32, depending on CO2 emission levels, representing increases of 15 to 100%, respectively, in hydrogen ion concentration. Decreased ocean pH threatens coral reefs, marine calcifying organisms, and their respective ecosystems.

 

 

 

 

 

 

The global temperature has increased 0.85o centigrade since 1880, and is on track to increase by 4o by year 2100 if no action is taken. CO2 levels are on track to increase to up to 900 parts per million (ppm) by 2100 from the current 401 ppm today. Furthermore, if behaviors aren’t modified the situation gets worse after the year 2100. “CO2 remains in the atmosphere for decades to centuries, so a decline in emis­sions takes time to affect concentrations. Temperatures lag concentrations: tempera­tures will continue increasing for a few cen­turies after concentrations have stabilized. And sea levels lag temperature reductions: the thermal expansion of the ocean from an increase in temperature will last 1,000 years or more while the sea-level rise from melting ice could last several millennia.” [3]

 

 

Impact

 

Global Impact

The world already struggles to provide adequate food, water, and sanitation for 2.5 billion people, a third of the global population. By the year 2100, the population is expected to rise to 10.85 billion from the current 7.31 billion, a 48% increase. Population growth alone will stress the world’s ability to supply food, water, and sanitation. Global warming and increased CO2 levels will make the task far more difficult.

 

 

The changing climate will cause more droughts and heat waves, more flooding (warmer air can hold more water), decreased ocean pH, and higher sea levels that on the whole will decrease the world’s capacity to provide food and water (and therefore sanitation). Droughts and high temperatures will reduce crop yields, and intense flooding will unpredictably wipe out crops in affected areas for seasons at a time. The increase in ocean acidification will affect fisheries, decreasing variety and catch yields. “Sea levels could rise by one meter this century,”[3] increased sea levels will destroy coastal arable land, and contaminate drinking water supplies with salt water.

 

The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum lasted 100K years, the onset 56M years ago was rapid, occurring within a few thousand years. There were widespread extinctions to marine and terrestrial ecosystems. Temperatures increased by more than 5 degrees Centigrade. [12]. We’re on a path to increase temperatures by 5 degrees in a few centuries. Because plants and animals won’t have time to evolve to rapid changes in the climate, “the speed and magnitude of change could condemn more than 50 percent of species to extinc­tion.” [3]

“From mid-century on, climate change is projected to have more negative impacts on crops and livestock across the country – a trend that could diminish the security of our food supply.” [14]

 

According to the US Department of Defense climate change is a “threat multiplier” because it has the potential to exacerbate many of the challenges it faces today.

 

“The impacts of climate change may cause instability in other countries by impairing access to food and water, damaging infrastructure, spreading disease, uprooting and displacing large numbers of people, compelling mass migration, interrupting commercial activity, or restricting electricity availability. These developments could undermine already-­‐fragile governments that are unable to respond effectively or challenge currently-­‐stable governments, as well as increasing competition and tension between countries vying for limited resources. These gaps in governance can create an avenue for extremist ideologies and conditions that foster terrorism.” [15]

 

“Over the last decade, the Federal Government has incurred over $300 billion in direct costs due to extreme weather and fire alone.” [22] Taking action on climate change now will reduce the cost of reacting and adapting later.

 

Actions

The United States, with 4.4% of the world population, is responsible for 20% of global carbon emissions, 23 tons of carbon per capita, annually. High Income countries, with one-sixth of the world’s population, are responsible for nearly two-thirds of the greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, and average 15 tons of carbon per capita. Given its CO2 emissions, the USA has a responsibility not just to act, but to also lead.

 

 

Inform

According to the Pew Research Center, 61% of US adults believe the earth is getting warmer, but just 40% think humans have caused the warming trend. Furthermore, only 48% of the respondents believe global warming is a major threat to the United States.

 

Educating US citizens about climate change is paramount. Political representatives will support what their constituents want. If citizens are indifferent or unaware of climate change, then public policy is unlikely to change. There’s lots of information about climate change available, but it needs to be messaged in a way that 80% of the population can easily grasp it. Without public support, changing taxes on carbon, for instance, to make cleaner energy more appealing, or to invest in clean energy research and technology are unlikely.

 

Invest

In the year 2100 the energy consumption rate is expected to be between 40 and 50 Terawatts, compared to 16 today. Globally, 82% of energy is from fossil fuels, 32% Oil, 29% Coal, 21% Gas; and the CO2 contribution is: 35% from Oil, 44% from Coal, and 20% from Gas. The USA energy mix is 81.8% fossil fuels, 35.4% Oil, 18.3% Coal, and 28.1% Gas. The world cannot continue to emit increasing amounts of CO2 and expect to adapt somehow.

 

The US subsidizes fossil fuels to the tune of $4.7B, and spends $21B on climate change compared to $612B (53% of the discretionary budget) on defense. The spending priorities are very short term. Thus, the need to educate the public and animate political representatives to see that climate action is needed now.

 

The USA has to end fossil fuel subsidies. Furthermore, carbon must be taxed to pay for research and technology investments in clean energy, clean water, and climate adaptation and preparedness. Furthermore, the USA has to recognize the incredible challenge the world faces and inspire the next several generations of scientific minds to develop creative solutions to climate change.

 

 

Conclusion

The Global mean temperature is rising despite long and short-term natural climatic cooling trends. CO2 levels in the atmosphere and the sea are higher than they’ve been in any time in the past 10 million years. The rapid rate of change also defies what the earth has experienced in the last 50 million years. If global temperatures and CO2 levels are not brought back into balance quickly we risk food and water supply shortages, human health issues, more extreme weather events, global migrations and instability, and even a massive extinction event. As a major contributor to climate change, the USA has a responsibility to recognize the potential consequences and drive the changes necessary to reduce global CO2 emissions and return the world to the natural climate cycle.

Orbital Parameters

Three variables of the Earth’s orbit—eccentricity, obliquity, and precession—affect global climate. Changes in eccentricity (the amount the orbit diverges from a perfect circle) vary the distance of Earth from the Sun. Changes in obliquity (tilt of Earth’s axis) vary the strength of the seasons. Precession (wobble in Earth’s axis) varies

the timing of the seasons relative to where the earth is on its elliptical path around the sun. For example, currently the Northern Hemisphere summer solstice occurs when Earth is closest to the sun, in roughly 6000 years the autumnal equinox will occur when Earth is closest to the sun.

[from http://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/Features/Paleoclimatology_Evidence/]

Marcott’s Temperature Chart Controversy

There’s considerable controversy about showing 100-year smoothed data along with instrumented annually collected data. No one credibly disputes either set of data, the issue is that the smoothed data don’t show annual variation. Is today’s climate just part of normal variability, and would the current spike disappear if smoothed? The spike won’t disappear if smoothed, but it would be dampened. The argument about comparing the two data sets misses the larger point that we should be cooling based on solar irradiation, but we’re warming instead.
